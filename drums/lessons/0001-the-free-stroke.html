Single-Stroke Speed

LESSON 01

The Free Stroke

Speed is not something you push. It's something you stop preventing.

Your mission: raise the tempo at which you can play clean, relaxed 16th-note singles. This lesson installs the single motion that makes that possible — and confirms your honest starting number. matched grip practice pad

Why you can't just "play faster"

Every source that seriously teaches hand speed — Bachman, Mayer, Igoe — starts in the same place, and it isn't a speed drill. It's a relaxation drill. The reason: at the tempos you're chasing, you don't have time to lift the stick back up yourself. The stick has to come back on its own. That return is rebound, and free energy — but only if your hand is loose enough to let it happen.[1]

A clamped grip kills rebound. Then your muscles have to do the return trip, you fatigue, you flam, you hit a wall. This is why most players plateau: they're fighting the very physics that would carry them faster. Tension is the enemy of speed.[2]

The move: the free stroke

What it is

The free stroke (also called the full or legato stroke): hold the stick loosely, throw it down at the pad, and let it rebound all the way back up to where it started — then just catch it there. One input of energy, reused. Bachman's image: you're dribbling a basketball.[1]

Three things make it work:

  • The fulcrum. Thumb and index finger form the pivot, with a small gap — not a pinch — so the stick can swing freely. Igoe's whole method reduces to three words: "it's in the fulcrum."[3]
  • Loose back fingers. The other three fingers stay in light contact but never clamp. They'll do real work later; right now they just ride.
  • Let it rebound to the top. Don't stop the stick low. A full, high rebound now is what gives you room to add fingers for speed next.

How the free stroke turns into speed

Here's the mechanism you're really training. At slow tempos, the wrist flicks each stroke. But past a certain tempo the wrist can't flap fast enough — so the fingers take over the pumping, while the loose rebound keeps recycling energy. Fast singles are a wrist-and-finger blend, never a clamp. The exact ratio shifts continuously as you speed up.[1]

That's the deep reason we start with the relaxed free stroke and not a speed burst: the finger technique that carries high tempos is impossible from a tight hand. Loose first, fast second.

Do this now (≈10 min)

  1. Feel the rebound, one hand. No metronome yet. Right hand only: loose fulcrum, throw one stroke, let it bounce back up, catch. Ten slow reps. Then left hand. Watch the tip return to the same height each time.
  2. Add the metronome, slow. Set it to about half your ceiling. Play continuous 16th-note singles (RLRL), every stroke a full free stroke rebounding high. Loose. If it feels effortless, it's right.
  3. Confirm your honest ceiling. Climb in +5 BPM steps. Your ceiling is the last tempo where the strokes stay even, high, and relaxed — not the last tempo you can survive. Most players' real ceiling is slower than they think. Write the number down.
