REF-01

The 30-Minute Single-Stroke Routine

Print this. Keep it on the pad. Every block is metronome-driven.

Goal: raise the tempo at which you can play even, relaxed 16th-note single strokes. Every minute here serves that one number.

Before you start — the two anchor tempos

Ceiling = fastest continuous 16th-note singles you can play clean and relaxed. Base = your working start tempo, ≈ 15–20 BPM below ceiling.

Fill in today:   Base BPM  ·  Ceiling BPM

The four blocks

#BlockTimeTempoFocus
1Free-stroke warm-up5 min ~½ ceiling Slow 16ths, high strokes, pure rebound. One hand, then the other, then together. Groove the motion — no speed.
2The ladder (main work)12 min Base → up 1 min continuous 16ths. If clean & relaxed → +5 BPM. Climb until it breaks; hold the last clean rung. That rung is today's ceiling.
3Speed bursts8 min above ceiling 3 bars at a comfy tempo, 1 bar of singles at ~2× (or +30–40 BPM), back down. Repeat. Teaches the nervous system to fire faster.
4Evenness cool-down5 min moderate Accent-tap or down-up. Listen for flams between hands. Give the weaker hand the extra reps.

Progression rule (how the number actually climbs)

Only raise your base tempo when a rung feels solid for a whole session, not for one lucky minute. Speed built on clean reps stays; speed forced past control plateaus.[1]

Stroke quick-reference

Free stroke (a.k.a. full / legato stroke)

Loose grip. Throw the stick down, let it rebound back up to where it started, catch it there. Like dribbling a basketball — you spend energy once and reuse it.[2]

Fulcrum

The pivot: thumb + index finger, with a small gap so the stick can swing freely. "It's in the fulcrum."[3]

Wrist-to-finger ratio

Slow = mostly wrist. As tempo rises past what the wrist can flick, the fingers take over to keep the stick rebounding. Fast singles are a wrist–finger blend, never a clamp.[2]

The one rule above all

Tension is the enemy of speed. If your forearm burns or your grip clamps, you are working against the rebound. Slow down, loosen, rebuild.
